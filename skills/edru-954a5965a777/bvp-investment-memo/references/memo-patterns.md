# BVP Memo Patterns: Voice, Tone, and Analytical Frameworks

Distilled from 19 real Bessemer Venture Partners memos. These are the recurring moves, framings, and voice patterns that make a BVP memo feel authentic.

---

## Voice and Tone

### First-person plural, confident, direct
BVP memos are written as "we" — the investing partner speaking on behalf of the partnership. They stake a position and own it.

Good: "We recommend", "We believe", "We are excited", "We signed a term sheet"
Bad: "It is believed that", "One could argue", "Potentially this could"

### Quantitative by default
Every claim that can be backed by a number, is. BVP memos are full of specific numbers: $132mm GMV, 81% YoY customer growth, 0.5% gross MRR churn, 13-month CAC payback. Vague language is a red flag.

Bad: "The company has seen strong growth"
Good: "Customers grew from 5,500 to 10,000 (+81% YoY) with MRR growing from $164K to $438K (+151% YoY)"

### Honest about concerns and gaps
BVP memos name the problems directly. They don't paper over gaps with optimistic language.

"We still uncertainty about the precise business model" (Wix)
"The company has no VP of Marketing" (Shopify)
"The market size has yet to be defined" (Twitch)
"This is probably not the ideal time to be funding an early-stage Web 2.0 company" (Wix)

### Conversational asides welcome
BVP memos include personal commentary, anecdotes, and internal references. They feel like a smart colleague talking to other smart colleagues — not a consultant's report.

"Shopify was my first lead investment as a partner, so I was incredibly nervous"
"I have been tracking the company for several months"
"We recommend watching their 2-minute introduction video"
"(note Jeremy's 'picking apart Craig's List' thesis)"

### Urgency framing (when real)
Many memos explain why BVP moved fast or why the timing matters:
- "We tried to pre-empt Yelp's financing plans with a term sheet last week"
- "We are competing against another firm which has agreed to a $90 million pre-money valuation"
- "We moved quickly and were able to convince the company to let us invest"
- "Mackey's data-driven sourcing efforts gave us an early indicator before any peer firms"

## Signature BVP Analytical Moves

### The "X of Y" framing
BVP loves crisp analogies that position a company relative to a known reference point:
- Auth0: "The Twilio of identity"
- SendGrid: "Twilio for email" (BVP's own analogy)
- Dropcam: "The iPod of Internet cameras"

Use sparingly — only when the analogy is genuinely tight. The reference must be a product the audience already understands.

### The portfolio roadmap connection
BVP always contextualizes investments within their existing investment thesis or portfolio. This shows pattern recognition and conviction.
- "This fits well with our developer roadmap" (Auth0)
- "This coincides with our consumerization of enterprise software thesis" (Shopify)
- "We have a roadmap for consumer security investments" (LifeLock)
- "This is part of our broader local advertising investment thesis" (Yelp)

### The "why incumbents can't" argument
Nearly every memo explains structural reasons why established players won't or can't win. This is the moat argument.
- Incumbents are focused on enterprise and can't move downmarket without cannibalizing margins
- Legacy vendors require specialized integration teams; the startup does it in hours
- Platform players would have to compete with their own customers

### The organic growth signal
BVP celebrates capital-efficient, organic growth more than almost anything else. Mention it prominently when present.
- "With limited marketing" (Shopify)
- "Without any marketing spend" (Fiverr: 60K transactions/month with 8 people)
- "PR appearances are basically free" (LifeLock: 150 media appearances)
- Capital raised before this round, especially if very low, is always mentioned

### The product experience test
Several BVP memos describe the investor personally testing the product:
- "We built all the functionality inherent in Grand Central in about 5 minutes while on the phone with the CEO" (Twilio)
- "Now would be the time to look at the demo video or beta site if you haven't already" (Wix)
- "The best way to get a feel for Pinterest's product is to try it" (Pinterest)
BVP expects partners to actually use the product. Recreate this energy.

### The bottoms-up market size
When TAM is contested or uncertain, BVP builds it from the ground up:
- Toast: "1M restaurants x average ACV = $X revenue opportunity"
- Mindbody: counts by segment (yoga studios: X locations x $Y ACV = $Z) to build to $80M addressable
- Medi Assist: India health insurance premium growing 35% CAGR, only 3% penetrated

### The "even if the big vision fails" floor
For Series B+ memos especially, BVP identifies a realistic downside floor — strategic acquirers, profitable-at-current-scale scenario, or defensible niche.
- PagerDuty: "Even without achieving this bigger vision, there are a large number of potential acquirers from IBM, HP, BMC, ServiceNow, Splunk, SolarWinds"
- LinkedIn: "limited downside given the nearly profitable existing business model and minimal invested capital"

### The retrospective annotation (the BVP signature)
The published BVP memos include blockquoted retrospective commentary — the investor reflecting years later on what they got right or wrong. This is a unique BVP publishing convention.

When writing a memo for analysis or learning purposes, add a "What We'd Get Wrong" section at the end that identifies 2-3 risks or assumptions that might look different in hindsight.

## Metrics Frameworks

### SaaS Metrics Hierarchy (from PagerDuty, SendGrid, Mindbody, ServiceTitan, Toast)
1. ARR / MRR (current run rate)
2. Growth rate (YoY or MoM)
3. Gross margin %
4. Logo churn (monthly %)
5. Net revenue retention (should be >100%)
6. CAC (by channel if possible)
7. CAC payback (months)
8. LTV/CAC ratio

### Marketplace Metrics (from Fiverr, Twitch, Yelp)
1. GMV / transaction volume
2. Take rate %
3. Buyer and seller counts + repeat purchase rate
4. Supply quality metrics (completion rate, satisfaction)
5. Organic traffic % (paid vs. unpaid)

### Consumer/Network Metrics (from Pinterest, LinkedIn, Twitch)
1. MAU / DAU and growth
2. Engagement metrics (pages/session, time on site, content per user)
3. Viral coefficient / referral %
4. Content producer vs. consumer ratio (Yelp's 1% producer rule)
5. Network density / geographic penetration

### Valuation Multiple Context (always compute this)
BVP always states the implied multiple: "14x June ARR" (PagerDuty), "4x annualized MRR" (Mindbody), "12x CARR / 17x ARR" (Toast). Always compute and state the implied revenue multiple. Compare to comps if possible.

## Common Risk Categories Across 19 Memos

In order of frequency:

1. **Market size uncertainty** — 11/19 memos explicitly question whether the TAM is as large as claimed
2. **Management/team gap** — 10/19 name a specific missing hire or first-time CEO concern
3. **Business model risk** — 9/19 (especially early-stage where monetization is unproven)
4. **Competition** — 9/19 (but always specific: "Google/AWS/[name]", not "competition generally")
5. **Churn / retention** — 7/19 (especially when churn is high for the category)
6. **Regulatory / legal** — 4/19 (LifeLock, Medi Assist, Rocket Lab, LinkedIn)
7. **Customer concentration** — 4/19
8. **Platform dependency** — 3/19 (Twitch on game publishers, Yelp on Google, Shopify on third-party apps)
9. **Technology / product risk** — 3/19 (Velo3D, Dropcam, Rocket Lab — all hardware/deep tech)

## Section Order Patterns

### Most common ordering (Series A):
1. Opening investment recommendation (no heading)
2. Market or Product (varies)
3. Traction / Financials
4. Go-to-Market / Customer Acquisition
5. Competition
6. Team
7. Deal
8. Risks
9. Scenario Analysis
10. Conclusion
11. Appendix (optional)

### Series B+ additions:
- "Original Summary" section (recap of Series A thesis, updated with current data)
- Detailed sales metrics section (pipeline, rep productivity, funnel)
- Customer diligence section (quotes from reference calls)
- Expanded financials section with P&L

### Early-stage / seed variations:
- Often no scenario analysis (too early)
- Product section is disproportionately large
- Team section may come second (before product) if founder is the bet
- Risks section may be titled "Risks / Additional Questions to Pursue"
