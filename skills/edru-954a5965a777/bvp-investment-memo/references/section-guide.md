# Section Guide: BVP Investment Memo

Every BVP memo follows a recognizable anatomy. This guide details what each section contains and how to write it, drawn from 19 real memos.

---

## 1. Opening / Investment Recommendation (ALWAYS FIRST)

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-3 paragraphs, no heading OR heading "Recommendation" / "Overview"

The opening paragraph is the most important. It does all of the following:
- States the investment ask immediately: dollar amount, round, pre-money valuation, BVP ownership %
- Names the company and gives a one-sentence description of what they do
- Leads with the single most compelling signal (growth rate, organic traction, market position)
- Provides urgency context if relevant (competing term sheet, time pressure, pre-emptive deal)

**Examples from real memos:**
- "We seek approval for BVP to invest up to $7mm in the Series A financing of Shopify, a provider of e-commerce software to SMBs."
- "We recommend that BVP invest $13 million in Twitch.tv at $53 million pre-money to own 18.3% of the Company."
- "Following PagerDuty CEO Alex Solomon's presentation to the partnership on June 16th, we signed a term sheet to lead the company's Series B with an investment of $19.6m out of a $27.2m round at a $180m pre-money valuation."

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not start with industry background or market size
- Do not start with the company history
- Do not hedge: "We are considering an investment in..."

---

## 2. Summary (optional but common at Series B+)

**Present in:** ~12/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-2 paragraphs summarizing the full memo argument

Used to recap the thesis for partners who skim. Includes:
- What the company does and why it's exciting
- The 2-3 key metrics that matter most
- The big bet / expansion thesis
- Named risks that have been considered and accepted

---

## 3. Market Opportunity

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-3 paragraphs. May include sub-themes.

Structure: name the market, size it in dollars, identify the tailwind, explain why now.

**Required elements:**
- TAM in dollars with a named source or proxy (Forrester, IBD, market transaction data, comparable companies)
- 1-2 macro tailwinds that make the market attractive right now
- Why incumbents or existing solutions fail to serve the market well
- Often: "BVP roadmap" connection — how this fits existing portfolio thesis

**Examples of strong market framing from BVP memos:**
- Yelp: "Local advertising is a $94 billion market in the US and $150 billion globally... Very little of this market has moved online."
- Mindbody: "Market size is the big issue" — BVP literally names the tension, then resolves it with a bottoms-up TAM
- Toast: "1M restaurants will transition to cloud POS in the coming decade"
- Medi Assist: India health insurance growing at 35% CAGR, only 3% penetrated by commercial insurance

---

## 4. Product

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-4 paragraphs. May include pricing table, screenshots, product walk-through.

Write this section as if explaining the product to a smart partner who has never seen it. Then explain the insight behind it.

**Required elements:**
- Plain-language description: what does it do, who uses it, how
- The differentiating insight: why this approach vs. alternatives
- Product experience detail: onboarding speed, pricing, key features
- Ecosystem / platform / API angle if present (this is a BVP favorite — Shopify App Store, Auth0 rules, SendGrid API)
- Pricing tiers if applicable

**BVP's favorite product framing patterns:**
- "X is the [well-known product] of [category]" (Auth0 = "Twilio of identity", SendGrid = "Twilio for email")
- The self-serve signup narrative: "A developer can sign up, pick a phone number, and start building in 5 minutes"
- The "built it ourselves as a test" — BVP staff actually tried the product and reports the experience
- The ecosystem lock-in: why the product gets stickier over time

## 5. Traction / Financials

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** Combination of prose + tables + charts (described in text)

This is the heart of any BVP memo. The depth of metrics scales with stage.

### Seed stage metrics
- MRR and YoY/MoM growth rate
- Customer or user count
- Organic vs. paid acquisition split
- Capital efficiency: how much raised to reach this point
- Any unit economics early signals

### Series A metrics
All of seed, plus:
- Gross margin %
- Monthly or annual churn rate
- CAC (cost to acquire one customer)
- CAC payback period
- LTV or LTV/CAC
- Revenue from top customers as % of total

### Series B+ metrics
All of Series A, plus:
- Net revenue retention (negative churn preferred)
- MRR expansion breakdown: new vs. expansion vs. churn
- Cohort retention curves
- Sales rep productivity (quota attainment, ramp time)
- Pipeline and bookings vs. actual
- P&L summary table
- Cash position and burn

**Specific metrics seen across BVP memos:**
- PagerDuty: 136% YoY growth, 0.5% gross MRR churn, 103-104% net negative churn, high 80s% gross margin
- Shopify: customers grew 5,500 → 10,000 (+81% YoY), MRR grew $164K → $438K (+151% YoY)
- Mindbody: 60% CAGR, 11% annual gross churn, 3.4x LTV/CAC, 13-month CAC payback
- ServiceTitan: $3.5M ARR, 6x YoY growth, cash flow positive with 20 employees
- Toast: 300% YoY growth, <1% quarterly gross churn, 8-month CAC payback, 75% gross margin

## 6. Customer Acquisition / Go-to-Market

**Present in:** ~15/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-3 paragraphs

Explain how customers find the product and why they stay.

**Required elements:**
- Primary acquisition channel(s) with rough % split
- CAC per channel if known
- Whether growth is primarily organic or paid (BVP strongly favors organic)
- Land-and-expand dynamics if present
- Sales motion: PLG, inside sales, enterprise, marketplace, word of mouth

**BVP's favorite signals:**
- "Zero marketing spend" (Fiverr, Shopify early) — organic growth is the ultimate signal
- "Developer community word-of-mouth" (Twilio, Auth0, SendGrid)
- "PR and media appearances only" (LifeLock — 150 media appearances, zero paid)
- Virality loops: Yelp's super-users, Pinterest's tastemakers, Twitch's streamers-attracting-viewers

---

## 7. Competition

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-3 paragraphs, sometimes a comparison table

Name specific competitors. Explain why each cannot win.

**Required elements:**
- Direct competitors named (not "there are many competitors")
- Why incumbents can't move downmarket or into this space
- Why the startup's approach creates a structural advantage
- Risk acknowledgment: what could change

**BVP patterns:**
- "The incumbent would have to cannibalize their own business model to compete"
- "They're focused on [large customer/complex segment], leaving [target segment] completely unserved"
- Twilio: telecom incumbents need specialized teams; Twilio needs 5 minutes
- Shopify: enterprise e-commerce too expensive and complex for SMBs
- Auth0: building IAM in-house requires 2 engineers + 1 month + ongoing risk of Heartbleed

---

## 8. Team

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-2 paragraphs

Write the team section as a character read of the CEO first, then co-founders, then named gaps.

**Required elements:**
- CEO: prior companies, domain expertise, leadership style, one-line character assessment
- Co-founders: technical credentials, domain fit
- Key hires made since founding
- Named gaps: "the company has no VP of Marketing" (Shopify), "first-time CEO who has never scaled past 25" — be honest
- Location and talent sourcing advantage if any

**BVP patterns:**
- Shopify: "young, first-time CEO who is thoughtful, has good product and management instincts"
- PagerDuty: CEO worked on Amazon ops team for 2 years — directly relevant domain experience
- Auth0: "Twilio of identity" framing carried through to team — developer-first DNA
- ServiceTitan: "hungry entrepreneurs who will work tirelessly"
- Don't just list bios — make a judgment call on the person

---

## 9. Deal

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-2 paragraphs or bullet list

State the exact financial terms.

**Required elements:**
- Pre-money valuation
- BVP investment amount
- Total round size
- BVP ownership % (fully diluted, post-money)
- Option pool size and whether included in pre-money
- Security type (almost always "straight preferred" or "1x non-participating preferred")
- Board seat(s)
- Any secondary / founder liquidity
- Co-investors
- Any special rights: pro-rata, ROFR, information rights

**Valuation framing:** BVP always provides a multiple context — "14x June ARR" (PagerDuty), "4x annualized MRR" (Mindbody), "12x CARR" (Toast). Always compute the implied revenue multiple and state it.

---

## 10. Risks

**Present in:** 18/19 memos  
**Format:** Bulleted or numbered list, 3-7 risks with brief explanations

This section distinguishes good memos from bad ones. The risks must be real, specific, and honest.

**Categories seen across BVP memos:**
- **Market size risk**: "SMBs are hard to estimate" (Shopify), "market size is the big issue" (Mindbody)
- **Business model risk**: "currently all revenue is $5 transactions" (Fiverr), "revenue model unproven" (Pinterest, Wix)
- **Founder/management gap**: "first-time CEO", "no VP of Marketing", "needs to hire a CFO"
- **Churn/retention risk**: specific churn rate that could worsen with scale
- **Competition risk**: "Google could build this", "AWS SES is the most dangerous competitor" (SendGrid)
- **Regulatory/legal**: LifeLock (FTC concerns), Medi Assist (India insurance regulation)
- **Platform/API dependency**: Twitch (game publishers could build this in-house)
- **Customer concentration**: "top 3 customers = X% of revenue"
- **Technology risk**: Velo3D (supportless printing unproven at scale)

**Anti-pattern:** Do NOT write "competition may intensify" or "market may not develop as expected" — these are not risks, they are truisms. Name the specific competitor, the specific gap, the specific scenario.

## 11. Outcomes / Scenario Analysis

**Present in:** ~15/19 memos  
**Format:** Table with bear / base / bull case

Show the return math. BVP always runs a simple 3-scenario model.

**Standard scenario table:**
| Scenario | Exit Year | Revenue at Exit | Exit Multiple | Enterprise Value | BVP Ownership | BVP Proceeds | Return Multiple |
|----------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|------------------|----------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Bear | Year X | $Xm | Xx | $Xm | X% | $Xm | Xx |
| Base | Year X | $Xm | Xx | $Xm | X% | $Xm | Xx |
| Bull | Year X | $Xm | Xx | $Xm | X% | $Xm | Xx |

Note: Use realistic multiples for the stage/sector (SaaS: 5-15x ARR; consumer: varies widely).

---

## 12. Conclusion

**Present in:** 19/19 memos  
**Format:** 1-2 paragraphs

One clear recommendation. The structure is always:
1. "We recommend this investment" — state it directly
2. The 2-3 core reasons (product, team, traction — pick the most important)
3. The biggest remaining question or condition to monitor

**BVP patterns:**
- Never end with "in summary, we have presented the above analysis"
- Always stake a position: "We believe X will be the defining platform for Y"
- Name the thing that would make the investment wrong

---

## 13. Appendix (optional)

**Present in:** ~8/19 memos  
**Format:** Supporting tables, charts, customer lists, additional financial data

Include: detailed cohort tables, customer references, additional market analysis, technical deep dives, financial model details. Keep out of the main memo body — reference from relevant sections.
